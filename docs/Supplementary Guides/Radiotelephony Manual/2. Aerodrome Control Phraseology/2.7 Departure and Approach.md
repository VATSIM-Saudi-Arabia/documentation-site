## 2.7.1 Radar Identification
Vectors may be assigned to confirm the identity of an aircraft. Other methods for ATS surveillance service identification consist of:

- **Utilizing position report information**

- **Requesting the aircraft to perform turns**

- **Using bearing and distance** (information from a prominent object or radio aid)

- **Transfer of control**

- **Use of SSR**

|                      **Controller Transmissions**                        |                                   **Pilot Transmissions**                                  |
|:------------------------------------------------------------------------:|:------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------:|
|             **"SAUDIA 123 FOR IDENTIFICATION TURN LEFT BY 030 DEGREES"**              |                                                                                            |
|                                                                          |                     **"LEFT BY 030 DEGREES FOR IDENTIFICATION, SAUDIA 123"**                     |

In case identification may be lost, or about to be lost, the pilot should be warned and given appropriate instructions

|                      **Controller Transmissions**                        |                                   **Pilot Transmissions**                                  |
|:------------------------------------------------------------------------:|:------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------:|
|             **"SAUDIA 123, IDENTIFICATION LOST, CONTACT JEDDAH CONTROL 126.500"**              |                                                                                            |
|                                                                          |                     **"JEDDAH CONTROL 126.500, SAUDIA 123"**                     |

## 2.7.2 Radar Vectoring
Radar vectors are employed to maintain lateral separation and to guide an aircraft onto its final approach course. Pilots must be briefed on the purpose of these vectors unless the intent is self-evident.

## 2.7.3 Traffic Information
Details concerning traffic on a converging course should be provided in this format:

- **Relative bearing of the potential conflict using the 12-hour clock**

- **Distance from the conflicting traffic**

- **Direction of the other flight**

- Any additional pertinent information, for example:

- **Opposite (or same) direction**

- **Crossing from left to right (or right to left)**

If available, the aircraft type and level, including if it is climbing or descending

Avoiding action must be issued by the controller if an imminent risk of collision is likely without an immediate maneuver.

## 2.7.4 Radar Vectors to Final
Radar vectors are provided to arriving aircraft to guide them onto a pilot-interpreted final approach aid, or to a location from which a visual approach may be initiated. In this instance, an identified flight is receiving radar vectors to the ILS.

The radar controller must inform the aircraft of its position at least once before directing it onto the final approach.
Pilots are to be notified if a controller plans to vector an aircraft across the final approach track, along with the justification for the track extension.

|                                 **Controller Transmissions**                                    |                                        **Pilot Transmissions**                                   |
|:-----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------:|:------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------:|
|                  **"FLYNAS 321, MAINTAIN PRESENT HEADING,TAKING YOU THROUGH THE LOCALIZER FOR SPACING"**                |                                                                                                  |
|                                                                                                 |                                      **"PRESENT HEADING, FLYNAS 321 "**                                 |

## 2.7.5 IFR Departures
If there is a doubt as to whether a pilot can comply with an ATC clearance or instruction, the
controller may follow the clearance or instruction by the phrase “IF UNABLE”, and subsequently
offer an alternative. If at any time a pilot receives a clearance or instruction which cannot be
complied with, that pilot shall advise the controller using the phrase “UNABLE” and give the reasons.

|                                        **Controller Transmissions**                                        |                                            **Pilot Transmissions**                                          |
|:----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------:|:-----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------:|
|                                                                                                            |           **"UNABLE SID, DUE AIRCRAFT EQUIPMENT REQUEST OMNIDIRECTIONAL DEPARTURE, FLYNAS 123"**            |
|                            **"FLYNAS 123, ROGER EXPECT OMNIDIRECTIONAL DEPARTURE"**                        |                                                                                                             |

## 2.1.7 Omnidirectional or radar departures
Some aircraft may be unable to fly an RNAV SID, or simply may not have the latest RNAV SIDs
installed into their aircraft. In this situation, controllers shall issue an omnidirectional departure. This includes:

- The **departure runway**
- A **heading** to fly after departure (normally runway heading)
- The **altitude** to climb to after departure
- Assigned **squawk** code
